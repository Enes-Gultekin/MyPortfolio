Iberian Peninsula: NDVI Trends & Land-Use Syndromes

Seasonal trend detection with Fourier regression and Seasonal Mann–Kendall, followed by a syndrome map to diagnose land-cover change processes.

Study Area

Iberian Peninsula

Tools

R, ArcGIS Pro

Core Methods

Fourier seasonality modeling, Seasonal Mann–Kendall, Zonal Statistics, Syndrome mapping

Project Overview

This project analyzes land-cover change across the Iberian Peninsula using NDVI time series. First, seasonal NDVI patterns are modeled and long-term trends are tested for significance. Second, diagnostic combinations of NDVI parameters (trend, phase shift, magnitude) are used to classify land-use syndromes that explain how and where land cover is changing.

Objectives

  • Model NDVI seasonality and quantify temporal trends across pixels.
  • Identify significant change areas using 5% and 10% significance thresholds.
  • Compare change dynamics across land-cover classes (e.g., arable vs semi-natural).
  • Create a syndrome map to interpret dominant land-cover change processes.
Figure 1: Significant pixels at 5% and 10% levels (green = significant).


Part A: Seasonal Trend Detection

Seasonal NDVI behavior was represented using Fourier polynomials (sine/cosine terms) within a linear regression framework to capture annual seasonality. Trend significance was tested using the Seasonal Mann–Kendall test:

  • p-value: indicates whether a pixel changes significantly over time.
  • Kendall’s Tau: indicates strength and direction of trend (−1 to +1).

Trend Significance Summary

  • 5% threshold: ~65.74% stable; ~34.26% changing.
  • 10% threshold: ~51.33% stable; ~48.67% changing.
Figure 2: Stable vs changing pixels at 5% and 10% significance levels.


Land-Cover Comparison (Zonal Statistics)

Zonal statistics were used to compare trend behavior between land-cover classes. Semi-natural land contained more changed pixels than arable land and showed a higher average Tau, indicating stronger positive vegetation trends.

Part B: Syndrome Map (Land-Use Change Diagnosis)

A syndrome map was created to interpret land-cover change processes using NDVI trend, phase shift, and magnitude. The classification includes syndromes such as increasing/decreasing biomass and productivity, irrigation changes, and crop change.

Figure 4: Land-use syndromes map for the Iberian Peninsula.

Dominant Syndromes

  • Increasing biomass (Semi-natural) — dominant pattern (~51.79%): consistent with vegetation recovery (e.g., land abandonment, reforestation).
  • Increasing productivity (Arable) — second strongest (~23.97%): consistent with agricultural intensification (fertilizers/irrigation).
  • Negative syndromes (decreasing biomass/productivity) appear only in small patches and are rare.

Results

  • Most pixels remain stable, but the share of changing pixels increases substantially at 10% significance.
  • Semi-natural land shows the strongest positive trends and the greatest amount of change.
  • The syndrome map highlights vegetation recovery as the dominant change process across marginal areas.
  • Agricultural productivity increase is the second major process, reflecting intensification patterns.

Conclusion

This analysis combines seasonal trend statistics and diagnostic syndrome mapping to move from “where change occurs” to “what kind of change is happening.” The Iberian Peninsula is dominated by increasing biomass in semi-natural areas and increasing productivity in arable areas, while negative change patterns remain localized and limited.

Key Insights

Seasonality-aware trend testing helps avoid false signals in NDVI time series.

Semi-natural vegetation recovery is the dominant land-cover change process.

Agricultural intensification appears as increasing productivity in arable land.

Syndrome mapping turns statistics into interpretable land-use narratives.
