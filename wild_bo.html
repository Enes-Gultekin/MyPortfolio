Project Overview

This project analyzes how environmental conditions and wildlife movement influence vineyard suitability in the Ruwer watershed near Trier, Germany. Using GIS-based modeling, the study evaluates optimal vineyard locations and assesses the potential impact of wild boar activity on vineyard abandonment.

Results

The integration of vineyard suitability modeling and cost-distance analysis reveals clear spatial patterns in both environmental suitability and wild boar accessibility.

Key Findings

  • Highest suitability is concentrated in the northern Ruwer watershed.
  • Low-cost movement corridors occur near forest edges and gentle slopes.
  • High-cost zones act as natural barriers to wild boar movement.
  • Least-cost paths range from 779 m to 14,789 m (mean: 7,937 m).
  • Vineyards in low-cost corridors are more vulnerable to wildlife intrusion.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates how environmental suitability and wildlife movement interact to shape vineyard viability in the Ruwer watershed. While climate, slope, and soil conditions remain the primary drivers of vineyard suitability, wild boar accessibility introduces an important ecological dimension.

The northern watershed offers the most favorable conditions and is naturally protected by high-cost terrain. Vineyards near forest edges or within low-cost corridors face increased wildlife pressure and may benefit from protective measures such as fencing.

Key Insights

Optimal vineyard zones cluster in the northern watershed.

Wild boar movement follows low-cost corridors near forests.

High-cost terrain naturally protects certain vineyards.

Vulnerable vineyards lie near forest edges and require protection.

Integrated modeling provides a holistic understanding of vineyard dynamics.
