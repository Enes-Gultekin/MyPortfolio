Botswana: Rainfall–Biomass Interactions with DLM + Random Forest

Distributed Lag Modeling (DLM) to detect delayed rainfall effects on NDVI anomalies and regression/classification to explain sensitivity with environmental variables.

Study Area

Botswana

Tools

R (DLM + Random Forest), Raster Processing Workflow

Inputs

NDVI anomalies, Rainfall lags, DEM, Aridity, LST, Evapotranspiration, Topographic Indices

Project Overview

This project examines how rainfall influences vegetation dynamics in Botswana, including delayed (lagged) effects on NDVI anomalies. A Distributed Lag Model (DLM) identifies where vegetation is sensitive to rainfall and which time lags matter most. A random forest model then explains spatial variability in sensitivity using terrain and climate variables.

Objectives

  • Fit a Distributed Lag Model (DLM) to quantify rainfall influence across lag months.
  • Identify “sensitive” regions using model significance/strength indicators (e.g., F-values).
  • Test which environmental variables best explain NDVI–rainfall sensitivity.
  • Classify significant lag relationships and evaluate model performance.
Figure 1: Sensitive NDVI–rainfall interaction areas .


Step 1: Distributed Lag Model (DLM)

The DLM was used to model how rainfall affects NDVI anomalies across multiple lag months. Spatial outputs were analyzed to locate sensitive regions and identify the most informative lags.

Key Observations

  • Sensitive areas (higher model response) were mainly located in the south-east of Botswana, with additional isolated areas elsewhere.
  • Most obvious rainfall influence occurred at lag 3 and lag 4 (months).


Step 2: Explaining Sensitivity with Random Forest (Regression)

The DLM outputs (F-values) were used as a target variable to explore relationships with environmental drivers. Terrain variables were derived from a DEM (e.g., slope, aspect, roughness, flow direction, TRI, TPI) and combined with climate/topographic measures (e.g., aridity, LST, evapotranspiration, TWI, vertical distance to channels).

Sampling & Data Cleaning

  • Random sampling: 25,000 training + 25,000 validation pixels (50,000 total).
  • Null values were removed to reduce bias and improve model stability.

Most Important Variables (Regression)

Variable importance indicated aridity as the strongest driver of NDVI–rainfall sensitivity, followed by elevation, land surface temperature, and evapotranspiration. Flow direction showed the lowest influence.

Figure 4: Random forest variable importance (regression)


Step 3: Classification of Significant Lag-3 Relationships

A random forest classifier was trained to predict whether lag-3 relationships were significant (5% threshold) based on the environmental raster layers. The same key variables remained dominant (aridity, elevation, evapotranspiration, land surface temperature).

Classification Performance (Key Numbers)

  • Non-significant pixels: ~96.73% correctly predicted (very strong).
  • Significant pixels: ~34.05% correctly predicted (weak; many false negatives).
  • The model tended to over/under-estimate class membership, reflecting imbalance and confusion in detection.
Figure 5: Confusion matrix of the random forest classification .

Results

  • Strongest rainfall influence on NDVI appears after 3–4 months.
  • Aridity consistently explains spatial differences in NDVI–rainfall sensitivity.
  • Regression explanations are strong, but classification struggles to detect the minority “significant” class.

Conclusion

The workflow links climate forcing to vegetation response using lag-aware modeling, then explains sensitivity using environmental context. Findings suggest that delayed rainfall effects are strongest at lag 3–4, and that aridity and energy/water-balance variables are key drivers. For classification tasks, improving minority-class detection would likely require balancing strategies, threshold tuning, or alternative metrics.

Key Insights

Lag effects matter: rainfall impacts peak after ~3–4 months.

Aridity dominates: strongest explanatory variable across models.

Terrain + climate context helps interpret spatial sensitivity patterns.

Classification is harder than regression due to class imbalance and false negatives.
